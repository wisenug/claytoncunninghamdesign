Process Notes

Icon grids and optical corrections

Clayton Cunningham · Lessons from the Autotrader and Kelley Blue Book icon systems

An icon set feels unified for reasons most viewers can't name: consistent visual weight, aligned angles, and shapes that occupy space the same way. None of that happens by drawing carefully. It happens by building a grid, and then knowing when to break it, because optical balance beats mathematical alignment every time the two disagree.

Reverse-engineer a grid from a quality icon set

For Autotrader's functional icons, I started from Heroicons. The set already had a strong mix of legibility and visual distinction, and covered core UI needs like chevrons, a settings gear, and a modal-close X. But extending a set by 40+ icons without a grid produces drift. So I reverse-engineered one from the existing style: measure the stroke logic, the corner radii, the angles, and the silhouettes of the icons that feel right, and formalize what they have in common. The grid was always there; someone just hadn't written it down yet.

The anatomy of a working grid

The Autotrader grid comes down to four rules. A 1px base grid keeps strokes crisp at product sizes. A 1.5px trim area at the edges stops icons from touching their bounding box, so tightly packed UI never feels crowded. Orthogonals sit at 90°, 45°, and 15, which is enough angles to draw anything, and few enough that diagonals across the set agree with each other. Keyshapes, the four most common overall silhouettes in the set, define how much area a square, circle, wide, or tall icon should occupy so they all read as the same size.

Keyshapes are why circles get bigger

A circle drawn to the same bounding box as a square looks smaller. It covers about 78% of the area. This is the whole reason keyshapes exist: the circle keyshape extends slightly past the square's edges so both shapes read as equal. The same correction logic applies everywhere: a triangle needs to sit lower than center to look centered, a heavy top-half needs a thinner stroke, and a diagonal stroke needs to be optically matched to a vertical one rather than mathematically equal. The grid gets you to 90%; your eyes do the last 10%, and the eyes win every argument.

Consistent weight is the real unifier

On the Kelley Blue Book set, the brief was clarity at every size across web, app, and editorial. What holds a set like that together isn't the shapes. It's consistent visual weight: the ratio of ink to empty space staying steady from the simplest icon to the busiest one. When an icon starts feeling heavier than its neighbors, the fix is almost never "make it smaller." It's removing interior detail until the weight matches.

Spot icons are a different instrument

Functional icons and spot icons share a family resemblance but follow different rules. Functional icons live within the UI and are often used on actual components in the design system, at a smaller size (16-32px). Autotrader's spot tier uses a duotone style of orange fills with gray strokes, rounded terminals, and generous negative space, because spot icons live larger, carry more personality, and can afford detail that would turn to mud at 16px. Define the two tiers separately and document where each is allowed to appear; most icon-system drift starts with a spot icon getting shrunk into a toolbar.
